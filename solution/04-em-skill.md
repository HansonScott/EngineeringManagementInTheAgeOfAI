# The EM Skill: A Strategic Partner, Not a Task Runner

*[← Custom Skills](./03-skills.md) | [Home](../README.md)*

---

The other skills solve discrete problems. This one is different.

The EM Skill isn't a workflow — it's a system for running the EM job itself. It encodes the full scope of what Engineering Management actually requires, organized into 22 workflows, grounded in my company's formal expectations for the role, and designed to surface the right question at the right time: *are you doing the things that actually matter?*

---

## Why a Dedicated EM Skill

Early in my Claude experimentation, I was using AI to do management tasks more efficiently. Schedule prep, note capture, status updates. The efficiency was real. But I kept running into the same uncomfortable question: efficient at what, exactly?

The problem wasn't that I was doing things wrong. It was that I had no systematic way to see the full scope of what the EM role actually requires — and therefore no way to know what I was missing. Any individual session looked fine. The aggregate picture was invisible.

The EM Skill was built to make that picture visible.

---

## The Structure

### 22 Workflows Across Three Pillars

The skill maps the entire EM job onto 22 discrete workflows, organized across three pillars:

**People** — the core of the role. Growing careers, coaching, managing performance, building psychological safety, handling engagement, navigating exits. The work that determines whether your team is developing, or just executing.

**Process** — the operational layer. Driving delivery, building team resilience, reducing friction, making decisions, navigating ambiguity, broadcasting status, managing capacity, leading through change.

**Product** — the quality and direction layer. Building products that work, owning the -ilities (reliability, scalability, security, observability), ensuring compliance and standards, managing costs.

Each workflow has:
- A clear definition of what healthy looks like
- Observable signals that indicate drift or gaps
- Criteria for moving between green, yellow, and flagged health states
- A companion state file that persists context between sessions

### The Health Registry

Every workflow has a health status — green, yellow, or flagged — updated every time a deep dive runs. A central registry tracks when each workflow was last reviewed and when it's next due. Cadences vary by workflow type: some are weekly, some monthly, some quarterly.

The registry makes the invisible visible. At any point, I can see which workflows are overdue, which have been yellow for too long, and where I've been under-investing. It's the difference between "I think I'm covering everything" and "here's the evidence."

### Signal Taxonomy

Not every meeting is worth the same entry in the tracker. The skill uses a defined taxonomy to distinguish signal from noise:

- **People signals**: career and growth observations, coaching moments, wellbeing flags, team dynamics
- **Process signals**: delivery health, blockers, process friction, decision quality
- **Product signals**: quality standards, delivery health metrics, technical debt patterns

After any meeting, the question isn't "what happened" — it's "what signals fired?" Tracker entries are signal logs, not summaries. Over time, the tracker becomes a navigable record of what's actually been happening across every workflow area.

### The Strategic Pulse

Every 14 days, the skill surfaces five strategic questions:

1. **Altitude check** — Am I operating at the right level? Where did I get pulled into IC work?
2. **People check** — Across my team, who's growing? Who's coasting? Who needs something I haven't given?
3. **Anti-patterns** — Am I covering for underperformers? Avoiding hard conversations? Playing peer instead of manager?
4. **Strategic intent** — Does my time allocation match what actually matters right now?
5. **AI era** — Am I actively developing my team's judgment for working with AI, or just letting it happen passively?

The pulse isn't a checklist. It's a forcing function — a structured reason to step back from the week-to-week and ask whether the direction is right, not just whether the work is getting done.

## The Bigger Idea

What made this skill worth building wasn't the individual workflows. It was what happened when they were assembled: a complete picture of the EM job that I could actually look at.

Before this system, I knew my role was broad. But broad is abstract. I couldn't tell you, looking at the last 30 days, whether I'd given enough attention to the people pillar vs. the process pillar. Whether the three team members I'd been most in contact with were the three who needed the most attention, or just the three who happened to show up the most. Whether the things I was spending time on were the right things, or just the urgent things.

The EM Skill doesn't answer those questions automatically. But it surfaces them consistently. The difference between asking yourself "am I doing a good job?" once a quarter and reviewing a structured signal log every two weeks isn't just frequency — it's quality. The question gets sharper. The answer gets more honest.

The efficiency trap from the [Philosophy section](../philosophy/03-the-efficiency-trap.md) is real. This is the system I built to escape it.
