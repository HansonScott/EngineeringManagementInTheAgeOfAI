# Objectivity as the Standard

*[← The EM Inflection Point](./01-the-em-inflection-point.md) | [Home](../README.md) | [Next →](./03-finally-the-data.md)*

---

I've read a lot of management books. I've had managers who made me feel like I could do anything, and managers who made me want to quit. I've studied the difference, taken notes, tried to reverse-engineer what separated the good from the bad.

What I couldn't figure out, for a long time, was how to actually know which kind of manager I was becoming.

When I was an individual contributor, I had no real way to evaluate my manager objectively. I just knew how I felt. When I became a manager myself, my own performance reviews were often the same: subjective, sometimes emotional, shaped more by my relationship with my superior than by any consistent standard. I was told I was doing well, or that something needed to change, but rarely with the kind of specificity that would let me actually act on it.

The missing ingredient, I eventually realized, was objectivity. Not harsh criticism, just a clear, consistent read on what "good" actually looks like and whether I was hitting it.

---

## Why the Standard Tools Don't Work

Code has immediate feedback built in: a failing test, a compiler error, a user complaint. Management has no equivalent signal. The gap between a decision and its consequence can be months or years. And the evaluation tools that exist in that gap don't fill it well. 360-degree reviews measure how people feel about working with me. Engagement surveys capture sentiment. Performance conversations with my own manager are shaped by my relationship, my visibility, my last quarter's wins. I've sat through all of them. The feedback was often more about how I made people feel than what I actually did, closer to a popularity contest than a leadership assessment.

What managers need is something different: an evaluation grounded in observable behavior and consistent standards. Not feelings, not relationships, not how well I presented in the last all-hands.

---

## What If

What if I had something that could watch, not to evaluate me at review time, but to guide me in real time? Something that could see where my attention was actually going, which team members were getting quality time and which were being quietly underserved, which responsibilities were being handled well and which were slowly accumulating as gaps.

Not a person. A person brings their own biases, their own relationship with me, their own good and bad days. Something more consistent than that. Something that could hold the full picture of the role and measure what I was actually doing against it, week after week, without fatigue or drift.

What if the standard didn't have to live only in my own head?

That was the question I couldn't stop sitting with. I didn't have an answer yet. But asking it clearly felt like the first step toward one.
